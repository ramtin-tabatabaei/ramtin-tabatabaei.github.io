abstract: "In human–robot collaboration, repeated failures are inevitable and can critically impact user trust and perceptions of robot intelligence. This study investigates how different failure types — Freezing, Planning, and Grasping — and the robot’s awareness of these failures influence users across repeated interactions. Using a collaborative Tangram puzzle task, we conducted a mixed-design experiment with 54 participants, manipulating both the failure sequence type (homogeneous vs. heterogeneous) and the robot’s level of failure awareness (none, partial, full). The results reveal that user trust and perceived intelligence are affected by both the current and previous failure, with homogeneous sequences generally causing less trust decline than heterogeneous ones. Robots that demonstrated awareness — either full or partial — were rated significantly higher in trust and perceived intelligence than those showing no awareness, particularly for grasping and planning failures. Qualitative findings further underscore the importance of failure severity and the robot’s recovery behaviour in shaping user perceptions. These insights offer design implications for creating more resilient and trustworthy human–robot interactions amid inevitable errors."

<section class="paper-showcase__panel paper-showcase__panel--overview">
  <h2>Overview</h2>
<p>This research explores the impact of repeated robot failures and awareness in collaborative tasks. Through experiments involving Tangram puzzles, the study evaluates how different types and sequences of failures affect trust and perceived intelligence.</p>
</section>

<section class="paper-showcase__panel paper-showcase__panel--contributions">
  <h2>Key Contributions</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>Demonstrated that homogeneous failure sequences affect trust less negatively than heterogeneous ones.</li>
    <li>Showed that robots with awareness of their failures are rated higher in trust and intelligence.</li>
    <li>Emphasized the impact of failure sequence and robot awareness on user perception.</li>
    <li>Provided insights into designing resilient human-robot interactions.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section class="paper-showcase__panel paper-showcase__panel--method">
  <h2>Method and System</h2>
<p>A mixed-design experiment involving 54 participants was conducted using a collaborative Tangram puzzle task. The experiment manipulated failure sequence type (homogeneous vs. heterogeneous) and robot failure awareness levels (none, partial, full).</p>
</section>

<section class="paper-showcase__panel paper-showcase__panel--results">
  <h2>Results Overview</h2>
<p>- Homogeneous failure sequences cause less decline in trust than heterogeneous sequences. 
- Robot awareness of failures significantly improves trust and perceived intelligence.
- Grasping and planning failures are particularly impactful if the robot lacks awareness.</p>
</section>

<section class="paper-showcase__panel paper-showcase__panel--discussion">
  <h2>Discussion</h2>
<h3>Limitations</h3>
<p>The study was conducted in a controlled environment with limited failure types and only three instances per session, which may not fully represent real-world scenarios.</p>
<h3>Future Work</h3>
<p>Future research should explore adaptive repair strategies in more complex real-world settings, longer-term interactions, and varying failure severities.</p>
