abstract: "Robots are prone to making errors, which can negatively impact their credibility as teammates during collaborative tasks with human users. Detecting and recovering from these failures is crucial for maintaining effective trust from users. However, robots may fail without being aware of it. One way to detect such failures could be by analyzing humans' non-verbal behaviors and reactions to failures. This study investigates how human gaze dynamics can signal a robot's failure and examines how different types of failures affect people's perception of the robot. We conducted a user study with 27 participants collaborating with a robotic mobile manipulator to solve tangram puzzles. The robot was programmed to experience two types of failures —executional and decisional— occurring either at the beginning or end of the task, with or without acknowledgment of the failure. Our findings reveal that the type and timing of the robot's failure significantly affect participants' gaze behavior and perception of the robot. Specifically, executional failures led to more gaze shifts and increased focus on the robot, while decisional failures resulted in lower entropy in gaze transitions among areas of interest, particularly when the failure occurred at the end of the task. These results highlight that gaze can serve as a reliable indicator of robot failures and their types, and could also be used to predict the appropriate recovery actions."

<section class="paper-showcase__panel paper-showcase__panel--overview">
  <h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The study focuses on understanding how gaze can signal robot failures in human-robot collaboration by examining the types and timing of failures and whether the robot acknowledges the failure. Participants&#x27; gaze patterns were analyzed to understand their reactions.</p>
</section>

<section class="paper-showcase__panel paper-showcase__panel--contributions">
  <h2>Key Contributions</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>Identified gaze dynamics as indicators of robot failures.</li>
    <li>Defined executional and decisional failure types in HRI.</li>
    <li>Analyzed effects of failure timing on human perception.</li>
    <li>Explored acknowledgment impact on gaze behavior.</li>
    <li>Contributed insights for improved HRI design.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section class="paper-showcase__panel paper-showcase__panel--method">
  <h2>Method and System</h2>
<p>A user study with 27 participants interacting with a robotic mobile manipulator in tangram puzzle tasks. The robot experienced planned executional or decisional failures, and gaze behavior was measured using eye-tracking glasses.</p>
</section>

<section class="paper-showcase__panel paper-showcase__panel--results">
  <h2>Results Overview</h2>
<p>Executional failures caused more gaze shifts towards the robot. Decisional failures resulted in less entropy in gaze transitions, particularly when failures occurred late in the task. Acknowledgment of failure did not significantly impact gaze behavior.</p>
</section>

<section class="paper-showcase__panel paper-showcase__panel--discussion">
  <h2>Discussion</h2>
<h3>Limitations</h3>
<p>The study involved a specific task with a single robot type, limiting the generalizability. The relatively small participant number might affect the robustness of the findings.</p>
<h3>Future Work</h3>
<p>Future research could explore a wider range of robot failure types and their impact on different collaborative tasks, as well as the role of verbal explanations in failure recovery.</p>
